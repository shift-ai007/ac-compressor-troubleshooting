# Electrical Safety for AC Compressor Work

Working around AC compressors involves voltages that can kill you. This guide covers the minimum electrical safety knowledge for anyone near an outdoor AC unit — whether you're a homeowner checking a tripped breaker or a technician doing diagnostics.

## Voltages Present in Residential AC Systems

| Circuit | Voltage | Amperage | Lethal? |
|---------|---------|----------|---------|
| Compressor power (single-phase) | 208-240V | 15-35A | Yes |
| Compressor power (three-phase) | 208-230V | 10-25A | Yes |
| Control circuit (thermostat) | 24V AC | <1A | No |
| Contactor coil | 24V AC | <1A | No |
| Capacitor (stored charge) | 240-400V | Discharge | Yes |

**The most dangerous component is the run capacitor.** Even with power disconnected, a capacitor stores enough charge to cause a severe shock or cardiac arrest. Always discharge capacitors before touching anything inside the electrical compartment.

## The Disconnect — Your First Safety Step

Every AC outdoor unit has a disconnect box mounted on the wall nearby. This is a pull-out switch or breaker that cuts power to the unit.

### Before any inspection:

1. Go to the disconnect box (gray box on the wall near the outdoor unit)
2. Pull out the disconnect handle or flip the breaker to OFF
3. Verify power is off with a non-contact voltage tester on the wires inside the unit
4. **Do not trust the disconnect alone** — always verify with a meter

### Why verification matters:

Disconnect switches can fail. The contacts inside may be welded together from years of arcing. The handle pulls out, you think it's off, but 240V is still live on the other side. A $20 voltage tester prevents this from becoming a life-threatening mistake.

## What Homeowners Can Safely Do

These tasks do NOT require opening the electrical compartment:

- **Visual inspection** — Look through the grille for debris, ice, or obvious damage. No tools needed.
- **Reset the breaker** — If the AC trips its breaker, flip it off, wait 30 seconds, flip it back on. If it trips again immediately, stop — call a technician.
- **Check the disconnect** — Verify the disconnect handle is fully seated (sometimes it gets bumped).
- **Check the thermostat batteries** — Many "dead AC" calls turn out to be dead thermostat batteries.
- **Replace the air filter** — A clogged filter causes the system to overheat and trip safeties.

## What Requires a Licensed Technician

These tasks involve electrical testing and should only be done by someone with proper training and tools:

- **Measuring compressor windings** — Requires a multimeter on the compressor terminals with power off
- **Checking capacitor values** — Requires a capacitor-rated meter and proper discharge procedure
- **Testing contactor** — Involves measuring voltage across the contactor with power ON
- **Amp draw measurement** — Requires a clamp meter around live conductors
- **Any work inside the electrical compartment** — Florida law requires a licensed contractor (CAC license) for HVAC electrical work

## Capacitor Safety

Capacitors are cylindrical metal components that store electrical energy. Even with the disconnect pulled and the breaker off, capacitors retain their charge.

### Discharge procedure (technicians only):

1. Confirm power is off at disconnect AND breaker panel
2. Verify with a non-contact voltage tester
3. Using an insulated-handle screwdriver with at least 20,000V rating, short across each pair of capacitor terminals
4. You should see/hear a small spark — that's the stored charge dissipating
5. Verify the capacitor is discharged with a multimeter set to DC voltage across the terminals
6. Reading should be 0V or very close

**Never skip the discharge step.** A charged capacitor delivers its entire stored energy in milliseconds — faster than your nervous system can react. You cannot let go fast enough.

## Common Electrical Failure Symptoms

| Symptom | Likely Electrical Cause | DIY? |
|---------|------------------------|------|
| AC won't turn on at all | Tripped breaker, blown fuse, bad disconnect | Check breaker: Yes. Open unit: No |
| Outdoor fan runs but compressor doesn't | Failed capacitor or contactor | No — requires meter |
| Compressor hums but won't start | Failed start capacitor or locked rotor | No — dangerous |
| System trips breaker repeatedly | Ground fault, shorted winding, failing compressor | No — call immediately |
| Burning smell from outdoor unit | Overheated wiring, failing motor, arcing | No — turn off and call |

## When to Call Emergency Service

These situations are dangerous. Turn off the breaker (not just the disconnect — the breaker) and call for same-day service:

- **Visible sparking** at any point in the system
- **Burning smell** from the outdoor unit or air handler
- **Breaker trips immediately** when reset (indicates a short circuit)
- **Water pooling near electrical components** — water and 240V are a lethal combination
- **Melted or discolored wiring** visible through the access panel
