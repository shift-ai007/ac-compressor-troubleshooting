# Refrigerant Pressure-Temperature Chart

## How to Use This Chart

When a technician connects gauges to your system, they read the suction (low side) and discharge (high side) pressures. Combined with the outdoor temperature, these pressures indicate whether the system is properly charged.

**Important**: These are approximate ranges for a normally operating system. Actual pressures depend on system design, metering device type, duct sizing, and indoor conditions. Only EPA-certified technicians should handle refrigerants.

## R-410A (Puron) — Current Standard

Most residential AC systems installed 2010–2024 use R-410A.

### Normal Operating Pressures

| Outdoor Temp (°F) | Suction (Low Side) | Discharge (High Side) |
|-------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| 75 | 110–120 psig | 220–250 psig |
| 80 | 115–130 psig | 250–280 psig |
| 85 | 120–140 psig | 280–320 psig |
| 90 | 125–150 psig | 310–350 psig |
| 95 | 130–160 psig | 340–390 psig |
| 100 | 135–165 psig | 370–420 psig |
| 105 | 140–170 psig | 400–450 psig |

### What the Numbers Tell You

```
LOW suction + LOW discharge = Low refrigerant charge (leak)
LOW suction + HIGH discharge = Restriction in metering device or liquid line
HIGH suction + LOW discharge = Compressor valve failure (weak compressor)
HIGH suction + HIGH discharge = Overcharged or condenser airflow restriction
NORMAL suction + HIGH discharge = Dirty condenser coils
```

## R-32 — Next Generation

Some new high-efficiency systems (Daikin, Mitsubishi) use R-32. Lower global warming potential than R-410A.

### Normal Operating Pressures

| Outdoor Temp (°F) | Suction (Low Side) | Discharge (High Side) |
|-------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| 85 | 110–130 psig | 270–310 psig |
| 90 | 115–140 psig | 300–340 psig |
| 95 | 120–150 psig | 330–370 psig |
| 100 | 125–155 psig | 360–400 psig |

**Note**: R-32 is mildly flammable (A2L classification). Equipment and tools must be rated for A2L refrigerants.

## Superheat and Subcooling

Pressures alone don't tell the whole story. Technicians also measure:

### Superheat (at suction line)
```
Superheat = Actual suction line temp − Saturation temp at suction pressure

Normal range: 10–20°F (fixed metering) or 5–15°F (TXV)

High superheat (>25°F) = Low refrigerant charge or restricted metering
Low superheat (<5°F) = Overcharge or flooding (liquid reaching compressor)
```

### Subcooling (at liquid line)
```
Subcooling = Saturation temp at discharge pressure − Actual liquid line temp

Normal range: 8–15°F (TXV systems)

High subcooling (>20°F) = Overcharge or metering device restriction
Low subcooling (<5°F) = Low charge or condenser issue
```

## South Florida Considerations

- South Florida outdoor temps regularly exceed 95°F from May–October
- High ambient temperatures mean higher normal discharge pressures
- Systems near the upper pressure limits on hot days are working correctly, not overcharged
- Ocean-proximity salt air corrodes condenser coils, reducing airflow and raising discharge pressure
- Systems on concrete pads in direct sun run hotter than shaded systems — a shade structure (maintaining airflow clearance) can reduce discharge pressure 10–15 psig

## When Pressures Indicate Compressor Failure

```
Signs of a failed compressor (based on pressure behavior):

1. Equalized pressures — suction and discharge read the same
   → Internal valve failure, compressor not pumping

2. Suction never drops below 100 psig (R-410A)
   → Compressor not pulling a vacuum, valve damage

3. Discharge pressure won't rise above 200 psig (R-410A at 90°F)
   → Compressor not building pressure, mechanical failure

4. Pressures fluctuate wildly
   → Intermittent valve leak or liquid slugging

5. Both pressures climb with loud noise
   → Compressor in locked rotor condition, shut down immediately
```
